Absolute Contraindications
CWIS · EAST
Shock / Ongoing Resuscitation
Hemodynamically unstable. Note: stable on vasopressors may still qualify — individualize per CWIS 2025.
Severe TBI
GCS <8 or intracranial hypertension persisting >24h. CWIS-TBI study: SSRF still ↓ pneumonia in non-flail TBI (OR 0.29) — reassess after 24h if GCS improves.
Rib fractures exclusively involving ribs 1–2 or ribs 11–12
Excluded from all prospective trials. CWIS/EAST agreement. Ribs 1–2 and 11–12 carry different biomechanical and neurovascular risk profiles.
Acute Myocardial Infarction
Anticoagulation requirement and surgical cardiac stress. Absolute per CWIS 2025 and EAST 2017.
Relative Contraindications
CWIS · EAST
Age < 18 years
Bone growth concerns; FDA exclusion for most plating systems. Case reports exist for severe pediatric injuries.
Significant Comorbidities / Frailty
Active malignancy, terminal illness, severe cardiopulmonary disease. Age ≥80 = higher risk per CWIS 2025 — though elderly may benefit more (less physiologic reserve to tolerate non-op).
Mild to Moderate TBI (GCS 9–15)
Evaluate individually. CWIS-TBI retrospective data: SSRF associated with ↓ pneumonia risk even in moderate TBI (non-flail OR 0.29, p=0.013) without hampering neurological recovery.
Unstable Spinal Fracture / SCI
Stabilize spine first. Paraplegia may still benefit from SSRF (intact chest wall sensation); quadriplegia benefit limited.
Empyema
Active chest space infection — hardware infection risk. Pneumonia alone does not contraindicate per CWIS 2025.
Prior Chest Wall Radiation / Pathologic Fracture
High hardware failure risk. Use 3D CT reconstruction to assess bone quality per CWIS 2025.
Therapeutic Anticoagulation
VTE, AFib, mechanical valve — not covered by acute MI contraindication. Assess reversibility: direct reversal agents (andexanet, idarucizumab), FFP/PCC for warfarin. Delay surgery until INR ≤1.5 or anti-Xa therapeutic range corrected. Denver Health PMG flags this as timing modifier.
Indication: Chest Wall Instability
CWIS · EAST
Flail Chest
≥3 consecutive ipsilateral ribs fractured in 2 locations ± paradoxical motion. Strongest evidence indication.
Offset / Bi-cortical Fractures
≥3 ipsilateral fractures with 100% displacement (no cross-sectional overlap) on axial CT. Updated CWIS/ASER 2025 taxonomy: "displaced" = no overlap; "offset" = <90% overlap.
Clinical Paradoxical Motion
Observed paradoxical chest wall movement on physical exam.
Instability / Clicking on Palpation
Crepitus, clicking, or instability on exam or as reported by patient.
📊 Flail chest: Supported by 3 RCTs, Cochrane review (Cataneo 2015), and Leinicke meta-analysis (Ann Surg 2013, n=431). Marasco RCT (J Am Coll Surg 2013): shorter ICU stay (285 vs 359 hrs, p=0.03), ↓ NIV requirement. Liu RCT (J Surg Res 2019): ↓ ARDS (28% vs 60%), ↓ pneumonia (48% vs 80%). CWIS 2025 and EAST 2017 both recommend SSRF for flail chest. LEVEL A

Additional Surgical Triggers — Denver Health PMG
≥30% Hemithorax Volume Loss on CT
Denver Health PMG (Pieracci): significant volume loss indicating severe chest wall collapse. Independent surgical indication even without classic flail pattern.
Large / Retained Hemothorax
WSES/CWIS 2024: hemothorax as co-indication for combined SSRF + thoracic washout. Particularly relevant post-chest tube. Retained hemothorax = frequent cause of re-admission in rib fracture patients (Denver Health data).

⚡ EAST vs. CWIS divergence: EAST 2017 found insufficient evidence to recommend SSRF for non-flail fractures and made no recommendation either way. CWIS 2025 and the Pieracci NON-FLAIL prospective trial (J Trauma 2020, Level II) now support this indication. This divergence should be acknowledged in clinical documentation and research.
📋 Pieracci NON-FLAIL trial (J Trauma 2020, Level II, 12 centers, n=110): SSRF within 72h improved pain at 2-week follow-up (primary outcome), ↓ pleural space complications, ↓ respiratory disability-related QoL. No significant mortality or vent-day benefit vs. non-op in non-flail. Pain and QoL benefits well-supported; mortality reduction not demonstrated. LEVEL B

⚠️ Failure-to-wean indication: Primarily retrospective data and expert consensus (CWIS 2025). Physiological rationale is sound but prospective validation limited. Critically confirm rib fractures are the primary driver of wean failure — exclude secretion burden, encephalopathy, diaphragmatic injury, and ventilator-associated complications before attributing to chest wall. LEVEL C
Timing Assessment
CWIS · EAST
Higher Priority Injuries Present
Open abdomen · Unstable spine requiring fixation · Pelvic external fixator precluding positioning · Significant vascular injury
CWIS 2025 Timing: Non-vent: <24h optimal, <72h acceptable. Vent/flail: earliest feasible. Vent/non-flail: <72h. SSRF >72h still indicated for flail/vent-failure. Delay if higher-priority injuries present.
Pieracci multicenter timing study (J Trauma 2018): <24h may be superior to <72h. CWIS 2025 reinforces this.
Morbidity benefit of early SSRF: Early fixation associated with ↓ ARDS (0.5% vs 1.5%) and ↓ ventilator-associated pneumonia (0.9% vs 2.3%) vs delayed fixation, and shorter hospital stay — even where mortality difference was not significant. This pulmonary morbidity reduction is the primary argument for early surgery in non-flail patients where mortality benefit is harder to demonstrate. Use this data when framing consent for non-flail, non-ventilated patients.
Multi-Guideline Recommendation
NON-OPERATIVE
Current data does not meet SSRF criteria. Optimize multimodal analgesia, locoregional therapy, and reassess.
⚠️ Pulmonary Contusion Note: Liu RCT (J Surg Res 2019) found that the vent/ICU LOS benefit of SSRF was not observed in patients with concomitant pulmonary contusion. Proceed with realistic outcome expectations — chest wall stabilization still beneficial for pain and mechanics but may not shorten vent course in this subgroup.
